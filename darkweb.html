Dark Web & Hacker Networks

The Internet was not born in a Silicon Valley garage. It was born in war. In 1969, the U.S. Department of Defense launched ARPANET,

a military communications network designed to survive a nuclear attack, connecting universities and research facilities under Pentagon funding. From that blueprint, the modern internet grew.

Decades later, the same military minds turned their attention to secrecy. In the mid 1990s, researchers at the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory developed Tor, The Onion Router, originally designed to protect American intelligence communications online. The idea was to encrypt traffic in layers, like an onion, bouncing it across the globe until its origin became untraceable.

The military eventually released Tor to the public. What was designed to shield soldiers and intelligence operatives from enemy surveillance became the backbone of the Dark Web as we know it today.

What is the internet?

When you visit a website or use an app on your PC or phone,

your data blasts through a massive web of routers and servers, connecting devices and networks across the globe.

Essential systems called DNS (Domain Name Servers) act as the ultimate translators.

turning the text you type, like google.com, into the raw computer IP address it belongs to, like 142.251.127.139.

What is The Dark Web ?

The Dark Web is a collection of websites and services inaccessible through standard browsers. Accessing it requires specialized software like Tor, which encrypts your traffic and bounces it through multiple nodes, network relays, hiding your real IP far better than a standard VPN.

Websites on the Tor network use .onion domains instead of .com or .net, and they can’t be accessed with regular browsers, only through Tor, which is built to reach these hidden services.

The DarkNet refers to the technology on which the Dark Web runs. There are also other hidden networks, I2P (Invisible Internet Project), which uses a method known as "garlic routing", and ZeroNet, but over 95% to 99% of the active, public Dark Web sites run on Tor.

The Deep Web refers to any part of the internet not indexed by search engines. This includes private emails, banking portals, medical records, and corporate databases, content that exists online but is deliberately kept out of public search results (can be accessed with regular browsers).

Who and what is on the Dark Web ?

It isn't just a playground for hackers and cybercriminals.

It is also a digital frontline for law enforcement and cyber threat hunters tracking them down, as well as journalists operating in hostile regions where anonymity is a matter of survival.

There can be found websites, facilitating, selling, advertising anything, from databases from databreaches, viruses bought by other hackers, to shops of illegal substances, weapons, and other darker crimes...

Some portals are either scams or covert websites set up by law enforcement to trap criminals. Yet, it isn't all crime. Major news outlets from the regular 'clearnet' also host identical versions of their sites here, providing critical access for people in countries where the standard internet is heavily censored and banned.

Things not necessarily crime related can also be found shared anonymously, but downloading anything from the Dark Web is strongly not recommended.

Botnets and C2

Botnet (short for "robot network") is a collection of hijacked devices, computers, smartphones, or smart home gadgets, devices in production lines, infected with malware. Without their owners ever knowing, these compromised devices become "zombies", controlled remotely by hackers. They are used to launch devastating cyberattacks, steal data, flood online services, and disrupt critical infrastructure such as factories and power plants.

C2 (Command and Control, C&C) is the digital headquarters, the server used by cybercriminals to remotely issue commands, deliver updates, and direct attacks across their infected botnet armies.

The Cyber Hunt

Sometimes, technical fingerprints left by Dark Web sites, the technologies they run on, their coding patterns, or infrastructure quirks, can be cross-referenced with data found on the clearnet. This correlation can unmask malicious websites linked to botnets, phishing email campaigns, and other criminal operations, connecting the hidden world of the Dark Web to its visible footprint on the surface web.

An interesting technique used in cyberattacks is the Fast Flux DNS, as firewalls and detection systems (IDS/IPS) filter traffic sent through unknown ports, hackers exploit legitimate services like DNS to secretly exfiltrate data undetected.

Fast Flux DNS is a technique where hackers rapidly rotate IP addresses linked to a domain, making their infrastructure nearly impossible to trace or shut down.

Security systems fight back by monitoring DNS traffic for suspicious patterns, domains that change IP addresses frantically, or with suspiciously short caching times. Once identified, these domains are sinkholed, traffic hijacked and redirected to servers controlled by defenders.
