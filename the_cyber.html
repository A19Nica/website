The Cyber

It started with curiosity. Then it became crime. Now it's warfare.

In the 1970s, the first hackers weren't criminals, they were curious programmers exploring the edges of what computers could do. By the 1980s, the first viruses appeared, and the world got its first taste of digital danger. The 1990s brought the internet into homes, and brought hackers into everything. Suddenly banks, governments, and businesses were targets.

The 2000s turned hacking into organised crime. The 2010s turned it into warfare.

Today, cyberattacks shut down hospitals, topple governments, and cost the global economy trillions every year.

This is no longer a niche technical problem. This is the war of our time.

The Hackers

Hacktivists hack for a cause. They are individuals or groups (like Anonymous) who use cyberattacks to promote a political, social, or environmental cause.

Organized Crime Groups hack for profit. Criminal gangs like REvil and LockBit use cyberattacks to steal money, extort businesses, and run criminal enterprises on a global scale.

Nation State hackers hack for power. Government-backed groups known as APTs (Advanced Persistent Threats) like Volt Typhoon (China) and Midnight Blizzard (Russia), who use cyberattacks to spy, sabotage, and wage silent wars on behalf of their countries.

Common Cyberattacks

Ransomware strikes by encrypting your files (the files are not readable, can not be used) making them completely inaccessible, and demanding payment.

DOS (Denial of Service) attacks flood your systems with traffic or run program operations on the computers until they buckle and shut down completely.

No device is 100% safe. Every major operating system, Windows, MAC OS, Linux, iOS (iPhone), and Android, has been targeted, breached, and compromised. No device, no brand, and no system is off limits.

Cyberattacks vary wildly in speed and sophistication. Some last seconds, a phishing email lands in your inbox, you click a link, and malicious code runs instantly, silently, without warning. Others last months. Complex attacks stage multiple malware infections, evading security measures, until data is extracted or entire infrastructures are shut down completely.

Supply Chain attacks strike by targeting external servers that communicate with the applications on your PC or phone. This injects hidden threats into trusted connections, making the malicious execution harder to detect but not impossible, as we will see in the next sections.

Cybersecurity, The Core Branches

Engineering builds the fortress. Before any hacker strikes, security engineers have already designed the walls, the locks, and the barriers standing between attackers and everything that needs to be secured.

Penetration Testers and Red Teams break the fortress on purpose. Before real hackers strike, penetration testers and red teams have already broken in, found every crack, every weak lock, and every unguarded door so they can be sealed before the enemy finds them.

The SOC (Security Operations Center), when an attack hits, it analyses every malicious file, tracing exactly how it got in, and tracking every suspicious operation it ran on the system and in the network.

The Fortress and The Traps

Among software used to block or track attacks are Antiviruses (and similar software like EDR, XDR, SIEM), honeypots, Decoy Accounts, and other software securing implementations.

Behavior based detection, monitors systems for anomalies rather than known threats. These systems flag unusual traffic volumes, activity at odd hours, or connections to suspicious hosts, similarly, unexpected user logins, unauthorized application installations, and other erratic behaviors are quickly detected.

DDoS Mitigation & Traffic Scrubbing Tools are deployed when a network is targeted by massive DoS flooding. Redirects the overwhelming traffic to globally distributed scrubbing centers. Here, malicious flood traffic is filtered out and discarded, while legitimate user traffic is safely rerouted back to the target destination, keeping the infrastructure online.

Honeypots are traps. Fake systems deliberately set up to look real, waiting for attackers to walk in. The moment a hacker touches a honeypot, defenders know exactly where they are, what they want, and how they got in.

Decoy Accounts are fake user accounts deliberately planted inside systems, waiting for an attacker to use them. The moment a hacker logs in with a decoy account, defenders know the system has been breached.
